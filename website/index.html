Income Gaps

Where are there income differences between groups within the same areas of work?
This visualization explores the shape of the United States labor market, examining differences in income between professions, gender, race / ethnicity, age, and education to better understand various pay gaps.
Skip / Start Intro >>
Below is the gap between the pay for males and females (using census gender definitions). The vertical line represents overall average pay. On the left, you can hover over (or click) to see that females make 12% less than the overall average while males make 11% more than the average.
<< Back / Next >>
However, when talking about differences in pay, how you frame the question is really important. If we expand our understanding to look per occupation, food preparation and serving sees a fairly small gap compared to the disparity in legal occupations.
<< Back / Next >>
Adding more nuance, clicking group size shows how many people are in each group by gender. Here, for example, "installation, maintenance, and repair" sees a smaller gap but a large disparity in participation by gender.
<< Back / Next >>
What about other types of gaps? Switch to "race and ethnicity". Notice that the "Hispanic" and "Black" populations see lower wages than the "White" population across many occupations.
<< Back / Next >>
Click "metrics" to note that the pay for things like "Farming, fishing, and forestry" compared to, for example, the "Legal" professions is lower. How might this interact with disparities in who holds those jobs? Note that "Pay" refers to average hourly pay and Gini refers to the Gini Index which is a measure of inequality (higher being more inequitable).
<< Back / Next >>
Of course, gaps may reflect differences in educational attainment. Click on edit filters and uncheck everything but "Advanced" and "College" under education. Then, click close filters. Note that, despite filtering down just to just college graduates, the same trend still holds.
<< Back / Next >>
However, income isn't the only way gaps emerge. Select unemployment with the filters still applied. In many (though not all) professions, those identifying as black see higher higher unemployment.
<< Back / Next >>
For example, while architecture and engineering sees only 1.7% unemployment, those identifying as black within that profession see unemployment at about 3% despite having similar educational background.
<< Back / Next >>
